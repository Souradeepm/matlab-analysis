Finding the Optimal Regularization Parameter in
Distribution of Relaxation Times Analysis
Nicolas Schlüter,
[a, b]
Sabine Ernst,
[a, b]
and Uwe Schröder*
[a, b]
The distribution of relaxation times (DRT) method allows the
direct interpretation of electrochemical impedance data, yield-
ing an increased resolution in the frequency domain. Calculat-
ing the DRT from experimental impedance spectra, however, is
an intrinsically ill-posed problem requiring special mathematical
treatments such as, for example, the Tikhonov regularization. In
this study, we propose a new approach for finding optimal
regularization parameters in the Tikhonov regularization. The
new test function is based on repetitive impedance measure-
ments and a simple data resampling with a subsequent
variance investigation. Furthermore, our new approach is
combined with already published procedures considering the
determination of best regularization parameters. Here, the
combination of several tests enables a clear assignment of an
upper and a lower boundary for suitable regularization
parameters. Finally, both tests were applied to simulated as well
as to experimental impedance data for validation. To analyze
the sensitivity of the approach, varying error extent was
generated to the synthetic and experimental measurements.
1. Introduction
Driven by the enormous efforts in battery and fuel cell research
and development, analytical methods for their in-depth charac-
terization become increasingly important. One of the most
powerful tools to investigate electrochemical systems is the
electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). Its non-invasive
nature and its ability to distinguish and identify different
physicochemical processes based on their characteristic time
constants make impedance spectroscopy an often used and
outstanding technique.
[1]
However, the interpretation of meas-
ured impedance data oftentimes requires a good knowledge
about the investigated system’s processes. Furthermore, when
the time constants of individual processes are close to each
other, data analysis can become complicated and ambiguous as
the latter’s signals overlap in frequency domain.
A technique facilitating an analysis without any a priori
knowledge about the system under investigation is the method
of Distribution of Relaxation Times (DRT). This method is further
associated with an improved data resolution in frequency
domain.
[2]
Thus, due to its potential towards advanced data
analysis, its application has gained popularity in recent
impedance research.
[3–9]
Nevertheless, the calculation of DRT
plots is an intrinsically ill-posed problem and regularization
methods are needed.
[10]
Therewith, new problems finding
optimal DRT solutions come along. An often-used mathematical
method is the Tikhonov regularization. Here, the central
problem is to find a proper regularization parameter, which
controls the extent of filtering noisy EIS data. In this work, a
new approach is proposed to find optimal regularization
parameters, which is based on a small number of repetitive
impedance measurements in combination with a statistical
resampling method. In addition, the new test function is
combined with a current approach of this research field. Finally,
the approaches are tested for simulated EIS spectra as well as
for experimental datasets.
2. Theory
A key problem in interpreting impedance data is to find a
meaningful model to describe the system under investigation.
As a consequence, an equivalent electric circuit (EEC) represent-
ing the system’s impedance behavior has to be constructed.
After finding a suitable EEC, consisting of passive electrical
elements, the model can be fitted to the measured data,
leading to a quantification of experimental values. For this
purpose, in 1977 MacDonald developed the Complex Nonlinear
Least-Square (CNLS) approach for the fitting of impedance data
to an appropriate EEC.
[11]
This expansion of conventional non-
linear least-square problems takes the complex character of the
impedance into account.
However, in general, the total number of physicochemical
processes, determining the investigated system, is not known a
priori. Therefore, ad hoc assumptions based on physical
intuition have to be made. Moreover, discrete process time
constants tend to overlap for most real electrochemical systems
and the assignment of an eligible model can get ambiguous. As

3. Results
3.1. Simulated Data
In order to determine the optimal size of the regularization
parameter, we combined the above-described cross-validation
with a new approach based on repetitive experimental
impedance measurements and the subsequent calculation of
variances.
A suitable l optimally leads to DRT results free of
oscillations. Therefore, the comparison of several DRT plots,
gained from repetitive impedance measurements, should result
in only small variances if the respective underlying impedance
spectra only deviate in stochastic error and the filtering works
well. Insufficient filtering (too small l value), in contrast, is
expected to provide diverging regularization outcome when
pristine impedance data is superimposed by noise. In that case,
this effect would be reflected by increasing variance of the
resulting DRT plots.

In Figure 6 (a), the two presented variance test results were

vs spectrum 2 and spectrum 1 vs spectrum 3). Although all

resampling was used to generate the curves in Figure 6 (b). The

results for a variance test with 20 simulated spectra and without
resampling. The latter perfectly fits the results which were
calculated with the resampling. The examination of even two
experimental datasets leads to stable test results if resampling
is performed. Hence, a random resampling of the available
experimental data should always be conducted, also because
such calculations are easy to automate with an appropriate
software tool.

Data Evaluation
All DRT plots were calculated using an extended version of the
MATLAB application DRTtools, developed and introduced by Wan
et al.
[10,31]
Our extension incorporates the automated calculation of
DRT plots with several regularization parameters. Additionally, the
test functions described above (variance test and cross-validation
test) were implemented. Furthermore, a software solution for the
calculation of the simulated impedance spectra mentioned before
was added to the GUI.
The DRT plots were calculated from the imaginary part of the
simulated and measured impedance data. Here, we assume the
validity of the Kramers-Kronig relations.
[32]
For the discretization,
Gaussian radial basis functions were used. The considered range of
time constants was obtained from the reciprocal impedance data’s
frequency range added by one order of magnitude at lower and
upper boarder. Afterwards, the number of time constants was
calculated by multiplying the amount of measured/simulated
frequencies with a factor of 10.
The standard procedure for the calculation of the variance test sum
was based on two simulated spectra or two measured spectra
respectively, which were resampled to 20 new artificial spectra.
These were also the basis for the CV. In that case, the synthetic
impedance spectra were averaged and eventually, the CV was
calculated for the mean impedance data. Furthermore, results of
the variance test and the cross validation were scaled for better
comparability. For the variance test, the test value at l ¼ 10
  15
was
set as base and all remaining data was divided by the latter. Cross
validation results were scaled by the test value at l ¼ 10
1
.
4. Conclusions
In this work, a new approach for finding the best regularization
parameters for generating DRT spectra was proposed. Repeti-
tive impedance measurements and the comparison of the
respective DRT plots offer an access for finding the minimum
required size of the regularization parameter. To keep the
experimental data acquisition as least elaborate as possible, a
software-based resampling of the measured data was intro-
duced, which allows the generation of synthetic datasets. Thus,
measuring a certain impedance spectrum twice gave enough
information for stable test values. Additionally, the cross-
validation introduced by Saccoccio, et al. was used to determine
an upper boundary for the regularization parameter. Both
methods in combination showed well applicability for simulated
as well as for experimental data. Here, the comparison of the
test results with the respective DRT plots was in good
accordance with the expectations, which were based on the
well-known information used for the impedance simulation and
measurement. Therefore, the combination of cross-validation
and new variance test offers a superior possibility to find
optimal regularization parameters in impedance deconvolution.
Naturally, this procedure is accompanied with additional
impedance measurements yielding in increased time expenses.
For this reason, it is advisable to use these tests only for initial
impedance spectra if the remaining spectra are investigated for
the same system and under comparable conditions.
In this work, in addition to the synthetic electrochemical
impedance data, only an electrical system was investigated
using the new test procedures. The application of the herein
shown proceeding for finding optimal regularization parame-
ters is still pending for real electrochemical impedance data.
Therefore, a stable and well-known electrochemical system has
to be examined by means of the new variance test and the
cross-validation test in future work. Thus, the test results can be
compared with the expectations for the respective measure-
ments and finally the practicability of the combination of both
tests for finding optimal regularization parameters can be
validated for experimental electrochemical spectra.
